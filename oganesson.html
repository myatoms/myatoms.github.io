Oganesson Atom Masterclass

Oganesson (Og) – The heaviest known element in the periodic table, with atomic number 118. It is a synthetic, superheavy transactinide element classified in group 18 (noble gases), but due to relativistic effects, it may behave more like a solid semiconductor and exhibit reactivity. Atomic Structure: Oganesson has 118 protons and 118 electrons, with the most known isotope having 176 neutrons, giving an atomic mass of approximately 294 u. The nucleus is massive and unstable. Electrons are arranged in seven shells: 2, 8, 18, 32, 32, 18, 8. In quantum mechanics, the outer electrons occupy the 7s and 7p orbitals, but relativistic effects cause significant deviations from lighter noble gases.

Isotopes: Only one isotope has been synthesized: ^294Og, with a half-life of about 0.7 milliseconds. Other isotopes are predicted but not yet observed. As of 2025, only five atoms of oganesson have ever been produced.

Physical Properties: Predicted to be a colorless solid at room temperature due to strong van der Waals forces and relativistic effects, rather than a gas like lighter noble gases. Estimated melting point: 45–53°C; boiling point: 107–113°C. Density: around 13.65 g/cm³ (solid). Highly radioactive and unstable.

Chemical Properties: Unlike inert noble gases, oganesson is expected to be reactive due to relativistic contraction of orbitals, potentially forming compounds like OgF₂ or OgO. It may behave more like a metalloid or even show metallic properties under certain conditions.

History: First synthesized in 2002 by a joint Russian-American team at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research in Dubna, Russia, and Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory. Confirmed in 2006 and officially named in 2016 after nuclear physicist Yuri Oganessian, who contributed to the discovery of superheavy elements.

Abundance and Production: Does not occur naturally in the universe due to its extreme instability. Produced in particle accelerators by bombarding californium-249 with calcium-48 ions: ²⁴⁹Cf + ⁴⁸Ca → ²⁹⁴Og + 3n. Production is extremely rare and energy-intensive.

Uses: No practical applications due to its short half-life and scarcity. Primarily used in scientific research to study the "island of stability" for superheavy elements, nuclear physics, and relativistic quantum chemistry.

Astrophysical Role: Theoretically could be produced in extreme astrophysical events like supernovae explosions or neutron star mergers via the r-process, but any formed would decay almost instantly. Not observed in space.

Quantum Significance: Oganesson is crucial for testing quantum electrodynamics (QED) and relativistic effects in atoms, as inner electrons move at fractions of the speed of light, leading to orbital contractions and unusual properties. It helps refine models for superheavy elements.

Biological Importance: None. Oganesson is too unstable, radioactive, and rare to have any biological role or presence in living organisms.

Fun Facts: Oganesson completes the seventh period of the periodic table and is the heaviest element known. It is the only element named after a living person (Yuri Oganessian) at the time of naming. Predictions suggest it might be a solid at room temperature, making it the only "noble gas" that isn't a gas. The element challenges our understanding of the periodic table's trends.
